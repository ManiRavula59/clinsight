Abstract
Retrieving relevant medical cases or documents is a critical information retrieval (IR) task in clinical decision support, particularly in cardiology, yet traditional search methods struggle with complex semantic queries in healthcare. Recent advances in large language models offer powerful language understanding, but LLMs alone cannot reliably retrieve factual cases due to knowledge cutoffs and hallucinations. We focus on a case retrieval task – given a textual description of a patient case, find similar prior cases or pertinent literature – formulated as a general IR problem rather than a purely medical study. Conventional lexical methods often miss semantic similarities, while static dense retrievers falter on out-of-domain medical vocabularies. LLMs can comprehend queries and context, but without external knowledge they may produce inaccurate or non-transparent results. We propose a novel LLM-augmented multi-agent retrieval framework that marries an LLM with dedicated retrieval agents in an iterative cooperation mechanism. Our method uses a LLM as a “planner” agent to reformulate queries and integrate medical (e.g., cardiology) context, and a retrieval agent (with a knowledge index) to fetch candidate cases; the agents interact iteratively, refining search and reranking results via a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) loop. This multi-agent design contributes three innovations: (1) an iterative query refinement strategy guided by LLM reasoning chains; (2) a cooperative retrieval architecture where an LLM agent and a search agent exchange information to improve relevance; (3) an LLM-based relevance estimator that grounds the LLM with retrieved evidence to mitigate hallucinations. Experiments on three open medical text datasets show our method outperforms baseline models by 5.3–6.1 percentage points in Recall@10 and NDCG, with statistically significant gains. We also observe improved generalization to novel conditions and robustness to query noise compared to baselines. The proposed framework, while validated on medical text, is broadly applicable to other knowledge-intensive retrieval tasks (legal case search, technical support archives), providing a foundation for intelligent IR systems that leverage both learning-based understanding and explicit retrieval for transparency and up-to-date knowledge.

In the medical domain, LLMs have shown the ability to encode a surprising amount of clinical knowledge (Singhal et al. 2023) and answer medi- cal questions at an expert level in certain evaluations (Singhal et al. 2023; Wang et al. 2024). This suggests they grasp domain semantics well. However, using LLMs alone for retrieval is problematic. LLMs are generative and lack direct access to up-to-date external knowledge, relying only on their training data (Ji et al. 2023). They often produce halluci- nated content – plausible-sounding but incorrect information (Bubeck et al. 2023) – which is unacceptable when query- ing for evidence in healthcare. Additionally, LLMs do not inherently return sources or document identifiers for their answers, limiting transparency. Simply asking an LLM to recall similar cases is akin to a “closed-book” approach and will miss relevant cases not seen during training. Therefore, LLMs cannot replace explicit retrieval engines when accu- rate and verifiable case lookup is required (Ji et al. 2023; Lewis et al. 2020).
A promising direction is to integrate LLMs with retrieval – harnessing the comprehension and reasoning abilities of LLMs together with the grounding of IR systems. Retrieval- Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques have emerged to incorporate external documents into LLM outputs (Lewis et al. 2020; Gao et al. 2023). By fetching relevant text snippets and feeding them into the LLM’s context, RAG can improve factual accuracy and reduce hallucinations in domains like healthcare (Gao et al. 2023; Zhao et al. 2024). For instance, Lewis et al. (2020) demonstrate that query- ing a knowledge base for supporting evidence significantly boosts performance on knowledge-intensive tasks. However, most existing RAG frameworks treat retrieval as a single-
step tool call: the LLM issues one query to a search module, gets some documents, and then generates an answer (Lewis et al. 2020; Yao et al. 2023). This one-shot approach may be insufficient for complex case queries that require itera- tive exploration. In medical case retrieval, a query might be initially broad or underspecified – e.g., “atypical chest pain case” – requiring follow-up clarification (Which features are most relevant? What specific context?). Humans often con- duct multi-turn searches, reformulating queries after seeing initial results. Recent work on LLM-based agents suggests that LLMs can emulate such interactive query reformula- tion. Yao et al. (2023) introduced ReAct, where the LLM interleaves reasoning and tool use, enabling multi-step query- answering. Similarly, Press et al. (He et al. 2025) and others propose letting the model ask itself follow-up questions (self- asking) and refine queries iteratively. These approaches hint that an LLM could guide a search process through multiple turns, acting as a high-level decision-maker or “agent” that decides when and how to query the data.
In parallel, the concept of multi-agent systems with LLMs is gaining traction (Shen et al. 2023; Nachimovsky et al. 2025). Instead of a single monolithic model, multiple spe- cialized agents (which could be instances of LLMs or a mix of models) can collaborate on sub-tasks, communi- cating in natural language. For example, one agent might specialize in planning and another in execution (Shen et al. 2023). In IR, a recent perspective by Nachimovsky et al. (2025) advocates modeling retrieval as interactions between query agents, document agents, and ranker agents. This is particularly relevant in dynamic environments where both queries and documents might be generated or analyzed by AI. A multi-agent viewpoint allows us to break the complex retrieval problem into modular components: one agent for- mulates refined queries, another fetches documents, another evaluates them, etc., all coordinated towards a common goal. Such a framework can potentially capture reasoning loops and negotiation that a single-step method cannot (Nachi- movsky et al. 2025). While multi-agent IR is still nascent, early explorations show improved performance when agents handle different aspects of the search process (Nachimovsky et al. 2025).
Considering these developments, we identify a critical research gap: existing IR methods (both classical and neu- ral) struggle with the semantic and generalization demands of medical case retrieval, and naive LLM integration (single- step RAG) does not fully leverage the LLM’s reasoning potential. There is a need for an innovative computing method that brings together the strengths of LLMs and IR in a more synergistic, interactive manner. Our work addresses this by introducing a cooperative multi-agent retrieval framework centered on LLM guidance, with the medical case domain as

2 Related work
2.1 Neural information retrieval and case-based search
Traditional vs Neural IR Early IR methods relied on lex- ical matching and probabilistic models. BM25 remains a strong classical baseline, scoring documents by term fre- quency and inverse document frequency (Robertson et al. 2009). However, BM25 cannot capture semantic similarity beyond exact token overlap. The advent of deep learning brought neural IR models that encode text in vector spaces to measure semantic relevance (Reimers and Gurevych 2019; Mitra et al. 2018). For instance, the Deep Relevance Match- ing Model (DRMM) introduced by Guo et al. (2016) applied neural networks to model term-level interactions for ad-hoc retrieval. More recent approaches learn end-to-end text repre- sentations: DPR (Dense Passage Retrieval) uses twin BERT encoders for questions and passages, trained on a contrastive loss to retrieve Wikipedia passages for QA (Karpukhin et al. 2020). ColBERT refines this with late interaction, comput- ing fine-grained term embeddings and achieving efficient yet accurate passage search (Khattab and Zaharia 2020)’. Extensive benchmarks like MS MARCO and BEIR have driven progress in neural IR, revealing that dense retriev- ers often outperform sparse methods on semantic matching tasks (Thakur et al. 2021). In the biomedical domain, neural IR has been applied to literature search and patient record retrieval. For example, BioBERT-based retrievers leverage domain-pretrained BERT to encode medical texts (Lee et al. 2020), improving over generic BERT on biomedical arti- cles. Despite their success, neural retrievers face challenges: they require large training sets of relevant query-document pairs, and performance can degrade when queries are from a different distribution than training data (the domain shift problem). In medical IR, labeled data is scarce and expensive to obtain, as relevance judgments need expert domain knowl- edge. Some unsupervised or zero-shot techniques have been explored, such as Contriever which uses contrastive learning on unlabeled text to build general-purpose dense retrievers (Gao et al. 2023). Gao et al. (2023) show that with careful pre-training (e.g. inverse cloze tasks and corpus-level MLM), zero-shot dense retrieval improves, but there remains a gap when tackling highly specialized queries.
Case-based medical retrieval Retrieving similar patient cases or relevant medical literature has been an area of inter- est in biomedical informatics for decades. Early systems used structured data or simple text filters to find matching records. The Text Retrieval Conference held a Clinical Deci- sion Support (CDS) Track (2014–2016) specifically targeting case-based retrieval: given a medical case narrative, the task was to retrieve relevant journal articles for answering clin-

 ical questions (diagnosis, test, treatment) (Tsatsaronis et al. 2015). Participants applied variations of BM25, knowledge- driven query expansion, and later, initial neural rerankers as BERT emerged. Simpson et al. (Kirk et al. 2016) and Roberts et al. (Kirk et al. 2016; Roberts et al. 2020) provide overviews of these efforts. Results indicated that lexical methods with domain-specific expansions (e.g., adding MeSH terms) were effective baselines, but complex narratives remained chal- lenging for automated systems. More recently, researchers have begun applying modern neural IR to clinical data. For example, dense embedding models have been used to identify similar patients in electronic health records for cohort study support (Miotto et al. 2016). Miotto et al. (2016) learned unsupervised patient representations (“Deep Patient”) from EHRs, which could be used to measure patient similar- ity, though their focus was predictive modeling rather than retrieval of records. Other works use deep metric learn- ing to explicitly learn a patient similarity function: for instance, convolutional neural networks over structured and unstructured data to cluster similar patients (Thakur et al. 2021; Devlin et al. 2019) (ref. patient similarity networks). However, these often treat similarity as a supervised clas- sification/regression (predict some outcome or match), as opposed to actual retrieval ranking. Our work differs by directly tackling unstructured textual case retrieval with a focus on generalization and using minimal supervised data. We also emphasize interpretability; whereas a metric learn- ing model might give a similarity score, our LLM agent can provide natural-language rationales (e.g., “both patients have a history of smoking and present with early-onset emphy- sema”), aligning with the need for explainability in clinical tools (Achiam et al. 2023; Touvron et al. 2023; Zakka et al. 2024).
2.2 Large language models and retrieval augmentation
LLMs in knowledge-intensive tasks Large language models like GPT-3 (Brown et al. 2020) and GPT-4 (Bubeck et al. 2023) have transformed NLP by demonstrating strong perfor- mance in question answering, summarization, and dialogue through few-shot prompts. However, for tasks requiring up-to-date or detailed knowledge (e.g. “What is the lat- est guideline for treating condition X?”), LLMs struggle because their knowledge is frozen at training time and may be incomplete or outdated (Ji et al. 2023). Researchers have thus explored retrieval-augmentation: combining LLMs with IR to ground their responses. Lewis et al. (2020) intro- duced Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), where an LLM-based generator is paired with a neural retriever to incorporate external documents into answer generation. RAG and related frameworks like REALM and FiD (Fusion-in-
Decoder) have shown significant gains in open-domain QA, fact-checking, and knowledge-intensive dialog by mitigat- ing hallucinations and improving factual correctness (Lewis et al. 2020; Gao et al. 2023; Jeong et al. 2024). In the med- ical domain, Singhal et al. (2023) presented Med-PaLM (an adaptation of GPT-4) and found that providing retrieval capa- bility could help the model handle questions it otherwise answered incorrectly. A recent survey by Gao et al. (2023) systematically reviews RAG techniques for LLMs, classi- fying approaches into naïve RAG (single-pass retrieve and read) versus more advanced iterative retrieval and modular generation. For instance, one study proposes iterative RAG for medicine, where an LLM can ask follow-up queries based on initial results (Zhao et al. 2024) – this resonates with our approach of multi-turn retrieval. Another survey by Zhao et al. (2024) discusses RAG for ensuring the provenance of AI-generated content, reinforcing that retrieval helps in gen- erating verifiable and trustworthy outputs. Our work builds on these insights, extending the paradigm by using the LLM not just as a passive consumer of retrieved facts, but as an active controller that drives the retrieval process itself (Pal et al. 2023; Xiong et al. 2024; Schick et al. 2023; Wei et al. 2022).
Interactive & multi-step retrieval with LLMs The idea of LLMs actively querying and interacting with tools has been explored in several contexts. The ReAct framework (Yao et al. 2023) demonstrated that an LLM (prompted suitably) can produce a sequence of reasoning statements and tool- use actions, enabling it to perform multi-step searches or API calls. For example, given a complex question, a ReAct agent might issue a search query, read the results, then rea- son and search again, emulating human research behavior (Yao et al. 2023). Similarly, Self-Ask prompting lets an LLM break a question into sub-questions and answer them one by one (possibly using a search engine for each) (He et al. 2025). These approaches showed improved performance on multi-hop QA and logical reasoning tasks by decompos- ing problems. In our scenario, a medical case query might implicitly be multi-hop (symptoms -> possible conditions -> similar case reports); an LLM that can iteratively search should handle this better than one-shot retrieval. Beyond single-agent prompting, recent systems use multiple LLMs or modules in a pipeline or loop. Shen et al. (2023) intro- duced HuggingGPT, where ChatGPT acts as a coordinator that delegates tasks (e.g., image analysis, speech recognition) to appropriate expert models. While their focus was multi- modal AI tasks, the concept of an LLM as a central planner is analogous to our planner agent. There is also growing inter- est in multi-agent communication: could two or more LLM agents converse to solve a problem? For instance, one agent could play the role of a “user” or “questioner” asking clarifi- cations, and another the “expert” answering, thereby refining

Park et al. and others have even simulated communities of agents to collaborate on tasks (e.g., Generative Agents) – albeit not for retrieval tasks (Nachimovsky et al. 2025). Our framework can be seen as a specific instantiation of this trend: we effectively create an agent that asks implicit questions (via query reformulation) and another that provides answers (via retrieval results), iterating until satisfied (Mialon et al. 2023; Lazaridou et al. 2022; Chen et al. 2017; Qu et al. 2020; Zhao et al. 2023).
Domain adaptation & generalization A notable advantage of integrating retrieval with LLMs is improved domain adapt- ability. Pure LLM solutions struggle with domain-specific jargon (e.g., medical abbreviations) unless fine-tuned exten- sively on that domain (Alsentzer et al. 2019; Gu et al. 2021). In contrast, retrieval can bring in actual domain text as needed. Domain-specific models like PubMedBERT (Gu et al. 2021) and ClinicalBERT (Alsentzer et al. 2019) were developed to address this by pretraining on biomedical cor- pora. They have been used as backbones for many medical NLP tasks and typically outperform generic models on in- domain evaluations. We leverage such models in our retrieval backbone to initialize semantic embeddings. However, even these models may not encode clinical reasoning out of the box (they learn language patterns, not how to connect a patient case to similar cases). There have been efforts to inject struc- tured medical knowledge or train models to cite evidence (Jeong et al. 2024). For example, Jeong et al. (2024) combine retrieval with a self-reflection step for medical QA, show- ing that retrieving relevant facts and then letting the model reason yields better medical reasoning accuracy. Another work by Nachimovsky et al. (2025) used retrieval to help an LLM explain medical answers in lay language, essentially augmenting domain knowledge. These indicate that hybrid approaches can achieve both accuracy and explainability, which purely neural end-to-end models lack. Our approach further emphasizes explainability: by having an LLM agent articulate why it is refining a query or why a retrieved case is relevant, we produce a trace of reasoning. This is important for user trust, especially in medicine where clinicians must understand the basis of recommendations. Prior IR systems for healthcare rarely provided such reasoning, other than sim- ple keyword highlighting. In contrast, our multi-agent system can generate a summary of how a past case is analogous to the query case, offering a form of case-based explanation (Formal et al. 2021; Santhanam et al. 2021; Nogueira et al. 2019; Dai et al. 2022).
In summary, our work is grounded in several strands of recent research: neural IR for semantic matching, LLM inte-
gration for knowledge augmentation, interactive multi-step retrieval, and domain adaptation in biomedical NLP. By syn- thesizing these directions, we aim to overcome their respec- tive limitations—such as the rigidity of traditional IR, the isolation of LLMs from external data, and the brittleness of single-step pipelines. While prior work has explored individ- ual aspects like ReAct-style reasoning, self-ask prompting, multi-agent retrieval frameworks, and LLM-based reranking, our method is among the first to integrate these components into a unified, cooperative multi-agent retrieval framework. We further validate this approach systematically on chal- lenging medical case retrieval benchmarks, highlighting its practical benefits in this domain. In the next section, we detail our methodology, followed by an extensive experimen- tal evaluation that substantiates the benefits of our approach over existing methods.
3 Methodology
In this section, we formalize the medical case retrieval task and describe our LLM-augmented multi-agent retrieval framework in detail. Our method consists of three main mod- ules – the Query Planner (LLM Agent), the Retrieval Module, and the Relevance Evaluator – that work together in an iter- ative loop. We first present the task definition and notation, then explain the overall architecture with algorithmic pseu- docode, and finally delve into each component’s technical design, including model training objectives and integration strategies. Key novel aspects, such as the multi-turn query refinement and LLM-based reranking, are highlighted.
3.1 Task definition and notation
We define medical case retrieval as follows: let D = d1, d2, . . . , d|D| be a corpus of textual documents represent- ing cases or articles (e.g. patient discharge summaries, case reports, Q&A snippets). For example, the LLM might output: “Document X is rel- evant because the patient described also had chest pain with normal coronaries, suggesting a similar MINOCA scenario.” These explanations can be presented to the user to increase trust in the results. While not a core requirement of retrieval, we consider this an important aspect of our framework’s design.
The above process is summarized in Algorithm 1 below. In Algorithm 1, the loop of steps 2–7 embodies the coopera- tive, iterative retrieval process. The LLM agent’s intelligence guides the search agent, while the search agent provides the LLM with grounding in actual data. This synergy is the crux of our framework’s innovation. Next, we detail the compo- nents: how the queries are refined (prompt design), how the retriever is built and trained, and how we train/integrate the LLM’s relevance evaluation.

One could also imagine optimizing this via rein- forcement learning on retrieval performance (reward = AP or NDCG of final results), but that’s complex; we left it for future work. Instead, we validate the efficacy of Qφ by the improved metrics after refinement (see Experiments).

3.5 LLM relevance evaluator and reranking
Once the iterative search concludes, we have a set of candi- dates C (T ) that are likely relevant. However, their initial ranks might not be optimal, because the bi-encoder is optimizing mostly for recall (it’s a shallow semantic match). Therefore, we add a final re-ranking step. Traditional IR pipelines often use a cross-encoder (BERT-based classifier that reads the query and doc together) to rerank top candidates with higher precision. We take a similar approach but leverage the LLM agent for this purpose, yielding both a score and an explana- tion for each document.
LLM Relevance Scoring: We fine-tuned the same LLM on a small supervised dataset for binary relevance classification. We used about 1000 query-doc pairs annotated (or inferred) as relevant or not (500 each) from a combination of:
• TREC-COVID and TREC-CDS judgments for some queries.
• PMC-Patients dataset’s similarity labels.
• A handful of expert-labeled cases we created for valida-
tion.

4 Experiments
We conducted extensive experiments to answer the follow- ing questions: (1) Effectiveness: Does our LLM-augmented multi-agent retrieval method outperform existing retrieval methods on medical case retrieval tasks? (2) Ablation: How much do different components (LLM query refinement, LLM reranking, hybrid scoring) contribute to the performance? (3) Generality and Robustness: Does the method general- ize across different datasets and remain robust to variations such as query noise or cross-domain application? (4) Effi- ciency and Scalability: What is the runtime overhead of the multi-agent approach, and is it feasible for practical use? (5) Explainability: Does the LLM agent provide useful explana- tions, and how do users perceive them (though this last point we address qualitatively)?
4.1 Datasets
We evaluated on three open-source datasets that cover a spec- trum of medical text retrieval scenarios:
• MIMIC-III Case Retrieval (Johnson et al. 2016): We derived a retrieval task from the MIMIC-III critical care database. MIMIC-III contains de-identified electronic health records for ICU patients, including discharge sum- maries. We constructed 100 queries by taking discharge summaries of patients as query documents (after remov- ing identifying info) and aimed to retrieve other patient records with similar diagnoses. We used ICD-9 diagnos- tic codes as a proxy for relevance: if two patients share a primary diagnosis code (e.g., myocardial infarction),
their documents are considered relevant to each other. On average, each query had 8 relevant records among a corpus of 10,000 summaries (we sampled a subset of MIMIC for manageability). This dataset tests case- to-case retrieval on long, unstructured EHR notes. It is challenging due to vocabulary (medical jargon, abbrevia- tions) and the length of documents (often >1000 words).
• TREC-COVIDIRChallenge(Robertsetal.2020):The TREC-COVID dataset was a shared task in 2020 with topics (queries) about COVID-19 and a corpus of scien- tific articles (from CORD-19). We chose this to evaluate our method’s performance on case to literature retrieval under a specific context. We used the 35 query topics from Round 5 of TREC-COVID (e.g., “impact of COVID-19 on mental health”). Although not patient cases per se, these are medical information needs expressed in natural language. The corpus has 60k articles. Relevant arti- cles per query were judged by TREC assessors (mean 40 per query). This dataset tests how our method han- dles broad biomedical questions and whether the LLM’s world knowledge (which might include some COVID info up to 2021) helps or causes issues. It’s also a test of handling a large corpus efficiently.
• PMC-Patients (Clinical Case Dataset) (Zhao et al. 2023): PMC-Patients is a recently released large dataset of patient summaries extracted from PubMed Central articles, with annotated relevance relations. It directly provides two tasks: (a) Patient-to-Patient Retrieval (PPR) – find similar patient cases; and (b) Patient-to-Article Retrieval (PAR) – find relevant journal articles given a patient case. We focus on the PPR task since it aligns with case retrieval. The dataset has 167k patient summaries; among them 293k pairs are marked as similar (derived via shared citations in the original articles). We sampled a subset of 5,000 queries and 50,000 corpus cases for eval- uation due to resource limits (ensuring each query had at least 1 relevant). This is by far the largest-scale and is a true case narrative retrieval task. Many cases are long and detailed, and relevance is defined by expert insight (e.g., whether two patients in case reports were consid- ered analogous by the authors). The size also tests the scalability of our approach.
These three datasets collectively cover a wide spectrum of medical retrieval scenarios: MIMIC-III focuses on EHR- based case-to-case retrieval; TREC-COVID targets topical medical literature search under time-sensitive public health crises; and PMC-Patients provides patient-to-patient narra- tive matching at scale. Together, they represent structured and unstructured data, formal and colloquial language, and nar- row versus broad information needs, offering a robust testbed without redundancy.

 Data preprocessing For each dataset, we preprocessed text by lowercasing, removing obvious de-identification artifacts (like “[Name]”), and splitting any very long documents (>1500 words) into multi-part documents (to avoid huge input length for BERT/LLM). 4.2 Baseline methods
We compared our approach (Multi-Agent LLM Retrieval, denoted Ours) with a comprehensive list of baseline methods spanning classical, neural, and LLM-based approaches:
• BM25 (Robertson et al. 2009): The standard Okapi BM25 ranking using Lucene’s implementation. This is a strong baseline in text retrieval, including medical IR tasks, and often hard to beat without large training data.
• BM25+RM3 (Robertson et al. 2009): BM25 with pseudo- relevance feedback (RM3) expansion. We expand each query with top 10 terms from the top 10 initial results (common setting). This often improves recall in medical search by adding synonyms.
• Doc2Vec (Le and Mikolov 2014): Unsupervised dense embedding of documents using the PV-DBOW model. We trained a Doc2Vec on each corpus (vector size 200). Queries are embedded by averaging word vectors (since Doc2Vec doesn’t directly give query embeddings). Sim- ilarity by cosine. This represents early neural IR without supervision.
• Sentence-BERT (MiniLM) (Reimers and Gurevych 2019): As a strong semantic baseline, we used a pre- trained Sentence-BERT model (specifically, all-MiniLM- L6-v2, a 6-layer 22M parameter model trained on general sentence pairs). We encoded query and documents with it and did cosine similarity search. This model is not domain-specific but captures general semantic similar- ity.
• BioBERT Dense (Lee et al. 2020): We fine-tuned BioBERT (BERT-base architecture) as a bi-encoder on each dataset’s training queries (where available). This baseline mirrors our retrieval model but without any LLM involvement or multi-turn ability. Essentially, it’s our bi-encoder alone. We include this to see how much

We used a publicly available checkpoint trained on MS MARCO, and then further fine-tuned it on our tasks (for those with training data). ColBERT uses contextualized token embeddings and max-sim scoring, which can capture finer relevance at the cost of index size. We evaluate it to see if fine-

4.3 Evaluation metrics
We primarily report Recall@K (with K=10 and 20), NDCG@10, and MAP (Mean Average Precision) for each method on each dataset. Recall@K is crucial in case retrieval because missing a truly relevant case could be serious. NDCG and MAP reflect ranking quality (NDCG@10 emphasizes the top results, MAP considers the entire ranking). We also measure Precision@10 to see specificity. For TREC-COVID (where relevance judgments have graded scores), NDCG is par- ticularly appropriate, but we treat it as binary relevant for simplicity (relevant vs not) in this evaluation.
Additionally, we perform statistical significance testing between our method and baselines using a paired t-test on per-query average precision, with p < 0.05 considered sig- nificant improvements.
4.4 Implementation details
All experiments were conducted on a machine with 4× NVIDIA A100 GPUs (40GB each), with total training time of approximately 20 GPU hours per dataset (bi-encoder train- ing). The LLM agent (7B parameter model) was fine-tuned using LoRA for 3 hours per task, and each query took on average 3–5 seconds end-to-end during inference, including iterative planning and reranking. Throughput during infer- ence was approximately 10 queries per minute when batched.
The average number of tokens processed per query (including prompt + retrieved text) was 800–1200 tokens per iteration. Given the cap of 3 iterations per query and a batch reranking of top-50 candidates, the total token consumption per query remained under 4,000 tokens.
These settings balance model performance with reason- able compute costs for real-world use.

Ablation B Removing LLM Reranker. We tested a version of our system where we do everything (including multi-turn refinement) but do not use the LLM for final reranking (and no sLLM in (7)). Instead, we simply take the top results from the bi-encoder (hybrid score) after the final iteration. The per- formance dropped by around 2 points NDCG and 3 points precision@10 across datasets. For instance, on MIMIC, with- out LLM rerank we got NDCG@10 ≈ 0.483 (vs 0.501 with it) and precision@10 0.50 (vs 0.54). The LLM’s value here is in fine-tuning the ordering at the top: it occasion- ally demoted false positives that the bi-encoder thought were similar but in fact had subtle differences making them irrel- evant (the LLM caught those by reading content). It also promoted some relevant docs that bi-encoder scored slightly lower due to vocabulary mismatch (essentially acting like a cross-encoder). Thus, the reranker LLM contributes, though its impact is smaller than the query refinement’s impact on recall.
Ablation C Disabling Hybrid (Using Dense Only). Our method uses hybrid dense+BM25 scoring for initial retrieval. If we remove the BM25 part and rely purely on dense scores for candidates, performance dropped slightly in recall (by 1-2 points) on MIMIC and PMC. The dense model, while trained, sometimes misses very specific terms (e.g., medication names or rare symptoms that are better treated as keywords). BM25 helped ensure those weren’t missed. However, the effect was not huge, indicating our dense model learned a lot of the term importance as well. On TREC-COVID, interestingly, dense-only was almost equal to hybrid, likely because queries were well-formed and models like BioBERT already know those terms.
Ablation D Varying LLM Agent Size. We experimented with a smaller LLM (2.7B parameters) versus a larger (13B) to see the effect. The 2.7B (fine-tuned similarly) resulted in about 1.5 points lower NDCG and noticeably worse explanations (sometimes incorrect). The 13B gave slightly better reason- ing but was slower; its retrieval performance was on par with 7B (within 0.3 points on metrics). We chose 7B as a sweet spot for speed and performance. This suggests that extremely
large models (like GPT-3.5 with 175B) are not necessary if you fine-tune a moderate LLM on domain tasks – a positive sign for deployability.
Ablation E Prompt Variations. We tested a variant prompt where the LLM was not asked to explicitly analyze results (just directly suggest a new query). That led to more erratic refinements, sometimes missing the reason to refine. Includ- ing a step where it “explains to itself” in the prompt yielded more consistent improvements. This aligns with chain-of- thought research that letting the model articulate reasoning leads to better decisions. We also tried an automatic stopping criteria by checking if top documents’ scores exceed a thresh- old (instead of LLM saying STOP). That was less reliable, as some queries needed stopping even if score threshold not met, because further refinement could have over-specialized. We kept the LLM’s own judgment for stopping, which worked well in our fine-tuned model (it stopped appropriately 90% of time; in 10% we noticed it could perhaps refine one more step but didn’t, which is an area to improve).
4.7 Statistical and robustness analysis
To assess the statistical significance of performance gains, we report 95% confidence intervals (CI) for core metrics based on bootstrapped sampling over test queries. For instance, our method achieves an NDCG@10 of 0.501 ± 0.014 on MIMIC, compared to 0.452 ± 0.012 for the best baseline (ColBERT), with a paired t-test p-value < 0.01.
Moreover, per-query breakdowns confirm consistency: across all 100 queries in MIMIC, 87 showed improved NDCG@10 with our method, and 61 queries improved by >5%. Similar trends were observed on PMC-Patients.
To evaluate robustness to iteration depth, we compared performance across different maximum iteration caps (T=0 to T=3). The gains mostly materialized within the first two iterations, with marginal returns beyond T=2. This supports our design choice to cap iterations at T=3 while ensuring efficient search.
4.8 Generalization and robustness experiments
Cross-dataset transfer We wanted to see if our approach, trained (fine-tuned) on one type of data, could apply to another without retraining – a measure of generalization. We took our model (bi-encoder + LLM agent) trained on PMC-Patients and applied it zero-shot to the TREC- COVID queries. Surprisingly, it still outperformed BM25 (NDCG@10 0.50 vs 0.447 BM25). It was slightly behind the model tuned with some COVID data (which got 0.535 as in Table 1). The LLM agent correctly refined some COVID queries by adding terms like “SARS-CoV-2” or known drug

However, for very spe- cific queries (like “effect of ACE2 polymorphism on COVID outcomes”), the model didn’t have training analogs and strug- gled to refine usefully. Robustness to noisy queries Clinical queries can be noisy or incomplete. We simulated noise by taking 20 queries from MIMIC and adding irrelevant sentences (e.g., “Patient’s favorite color is blue.”) or typos (random character swaps) to them. We then ran BM25 and our method on these perturbed queries. BM25’s precision dropped significantly (it got dis- tracted by odd terms like “blue”, retrieving some unrelated docs), and recall dropped slightly. Our method’s LLM agent was generally able to ignore irrelevant text; in its analysis it often didn’t even mention the added sentence, focusing on medical content. As a result, our performance remained within 1% of original on those queries, whereas BM25’s NDCG fell by 5%. This highlights that the LLM provides a degree of robustness to noise, effectively filtering out irrele- vant information from the query. This is a practical advantage in real-world use where queries may contain extraneous info or user errors.
Robustness to Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) terms We also tested queries containing a made-up drug name and a rare disease acronym that wasn’t in training data. The LLM agent asked (in refinement) for clarification of the acronym in one case, essentially splitting it out (not that it can truly clarify with user, but it rephrased query to use full form it guessed). For the made-up drug, both BM25 and our method couldn’t find it (no doc had it), but our method still retrieved rele- vant docs by focusing on the rest of query, whereas BM25 returned nothing in top ranks because it was too anchored on the rare term. This again demonstrates the resilience provided by semantic understanding and flexible reasoning.
Efficiency On a single Nvidia A100 GPU, our end-to-end query processing took on average 2.8 seconds (including up to 2 LLM calls and all retrieval steps) for MIMIC queries, and 3.5 seconds for PMC queries (which have longer text
to embed and more to rerank). Baseline neural methods like ColBERT took 1.5 seconds (mostly embedding + nearest neighbor search with no heavy model afterward). So our approach is slower, but still interactive for moderate use and could be optimized (e.g., pipeline the retrieval and LLM com- putations, use quantization on LLM). Notably, using GPT-3.5 via API was much slower ( 10–15 s per query) and costlier; our fine-tuned local LLM was more efficient. There is a trade- off: multi-step reasoning will inherently add latency, but we believe the accuracy gains justify it for scenarios where thor- ough retrieval is needed (like clinical research, where a few seconds per query is acceptable). In a live clinical setting with high frequency queries, further optimization or distilla- tion would be needed.

 5 Conclusions
We introduced a novel LLM-augmented multi-agent retrieval framework that advances the state-of-the-art in textual case retrieval, using medical scenarios as a challenging testbed. Unlike traditional retrieval systems or one-shot RAG approaches, our method features a cooperative interaction between an LLM agent and a search agent, enabling iterative query understanding, refinement, and evidence integration. Through this design, we effectively address key limitations of prior methods: the LLM agent’s reasoning mitigates semantic gaps and query ambiguity, while the retrieval agent grounds the process in factual data to prevent hallucinations.
Our experiments on multiple benchmarks (clinical notes, biomedical literature, case reports) demonstrate substantial performance improvements (4–8% absolute gains in ranking metrics) over strong baselines including fine-tuned neural retrievers and off-the-shelf ChatGPT-based pipelines. These gains are attributed to improved recall (finding relevant infor- mation that lexical or static models miss) and improved precision (the LLM’s ability to filter out false positives by actually “reading” content). We showed that each component – iterative querying, hybrid retrieval, and LLM reranking – contributes to the overall result, and removing any piece degrades performance. Notably, even in zero-shot settings and with noisy inputs, our system proved robust, highlight- ing the value of its design for real-world usage where queries may be imperfect.
In terms of innovation, our work is firmly rooted in com- puter science: we advanced methodologies in information retrieval (with a new multi-step retrieval algorithm, formal- ized with algorithms and mathematical models) and natural language processing (with an intelligent use of LLMs beyond end-to-end generation). The medical scenario served to val- idate the method’s efficacy in a high-stakes domain, but the core techniques are general. For instance, the same approach could be applied to legal case retrieval (an attorney find- ing past cases similar to a current one), or technical support (retrieving similar bug reports or Q&A threads), or any domain where queries are complex and data is extensive. The fact that our model can incorporate domain knowledge via retrieval rather than requiring huge domain-specific training makes it attractive for low-data domains as well.
Future work There are several promising directions to extend this research. First, we plan to explore multi-modal case retrieval – e.g., retrieving not only text but also related medical images (X-rays, MRIs) for a case. Our agent framework could be expanded with a vision model agent, orchestrated by the LLM (similar to how HuggingGPT links multimodal model). Second, we aim to integrate a knowl- edge graph of medical concepts as an additional tool for the LLM agent. This could allow the agent to verify or query spe- cific structured knowledge (for example, checking a drug’s
indication) during retrieval, further enhancing precision and explainability. Third, scalability optimization will be impor- tant: techniques like indexing LLM’s reasoning patterns or caching query refinements for similar queries could reduce latency. We will also investigate training a smaller student model to emulate the LLM agent’s behavior (model distilla- tion), to deploy in resource-constrained settings. Moreover, in order to make the framework viable in real-time clin- ical workflows — where low latency and high reliability are essential — we aim to investigate latency-bounded retrieval strategies. This includes limiting the number of agent iterations dynamically based on time budget, lever- aging lightweight retrieval approximators, and exploring distillation of the LLM agent into fast, edge-deployable variants. Future work will evaluate the framework’s respon- siveness under time-constrained settings such as emergency triage or ICU decision support, where milliseconds matter. We also consider asynchronous or parallel agent execution as a way to meet throughput demands in high-volume hospital systems.
Another avenue is employing reinforcement learning to fine-tune the LLM agent’s query strategy, using retrieval performance as reward. This could automate the refinement policy beyond what we supervised it for, possibly discover- ing even better strategies (like when to branch into multiple related queries). Ensuring ethical and fair use is also crucial; we need to monitor if the LLM agent exhibits any bias in how it picks results (e.g., overly favoring cases it “remembers” from training data which might not be the most relevant). By keeping the human-in-the-loop (through explanations and transparent sources), our system can facilitate auditing and corrections.
In conclusion, our work demonstrates a viable path towards next-generation retrieval systems that are not just sta- tistical matching engines, but intelligent, interactive agents. By leveraging LLMs for their reasoning and comprehen- sion in concert with traditional IR for grounding, we achieve superior performance and provide user-friendly outputs (with rationales). We believe this paradigm can greatly enhance retrieval tasks in many domains. As LLM technology evolves (e.g., newer models with even better reasoning or domain tuning), the efficacy of such multi-agent systems is poised to further improve, paving the way for retrieval systems that can understand, explain, and adapt – key features for trustworthy AI in critical applications like healthcare and beyond.
